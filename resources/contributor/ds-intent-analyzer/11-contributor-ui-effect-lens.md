# UI Effect Lens v1

## Purpose

This note defines the lightweight perceived-effect lens used by `ds-intent-analyzer`.

In this repo, “basic design psychology” should not mean:
- a textbook framework
- a list of universal laws
- a replacement for evidence-based product reading

It should mean:
- practical perceived-effect cues
- consequence-aware explanation
- better articulation of why a design decision helps or hurts the product experience

---

## Core rule

Use the effect lens to explain the likely consequence of a decision.
Do not use it as the primary decision engine.

The primary decision engine remains:
- constraints
- repeated jobs
- posture
- pattern pressure
- evidence quality

The effect lens only helps explain:
- what users may perceive
- what becomes easier or harder
- what trust, clarity, calm, harshness, precision, or noise signal is being amplified

---

## Allowed usage

Use this lens for:
- explanation aid
- audit sharpening
- anti-sameness reasoning
- more concrete “why this matters” guidance
- translating UI detail into product consequences for developers

Good examples:
- softer shape language may feel more approachable, but may also reduce perceived precision in a high-control workflow
- denser spacing may improve throughput, but may also increase scan effort if hierarchy is weak
- flatter visual emphasis may reduce noise, but may also flatten decision priority and make the UI feel generic

---

## Disallowed usage

Do not use this lens as:
- a universal law generator
- a shortcut that overrides product context
- a replacement for posture or archetype reasoning
- a reason to sound certain when evidence is weak
- a decorative UX-theory dump

Bad examples:
- rounded corners always improve usability
- stronger contrast is always better
- motion always improves polish
- one visual cue is enough to infer the whole system direction

---

## What this lens is best for

This lens is strongest when the skill needs to explain:
- why something feels generic
- why something feels noisy or harsh
- why something feels calm but less precise
- why something scans slowly
- why a trust-sensitive product may need different emphasis than a brand-led one

It is not for deciding the whole system from one cue.

---

## Practical cue examples

### Radius / shape
Can affect:
- softness
- severity
- approachability
- control

### Spacing
Can affect:
- grouping clarity
- calm vs crowding
- perceived effort
- cognitive load

### Typography
Can affect:
- readability
- scan speed
- seriousness
- effort to parse dense information

### Border / stroke
Can affect:
- precision
- rigidity
- quiet structure
- harshness

### Elevation / shadow
Can affect:
- layering clarity
- confidence
- polish
- noise risk

### Motion
Can affect:
- continuity
- perceived polish
- distraction risk
- sense of control

---

## KISS rule

If this lens makes the answer:
- longer
- more academic
- more generic
- less tied to product reality

then it is being used incorrectly.

The right output should still feel:
- concise
- practical
- consequence-aware
- product-fit-first
